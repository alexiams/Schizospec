Antipsychotics

  • Post by Alexia Walgren
  • June 3rd, 2020

Antipsychotics are a class of medication that can help with symptoms of psychosis. Most of these medications primarily help with positive symptoms of psychosis (definition here). There are two main types of antipsychotics; First generation antipsychotics (first used in the 50s) have potential to cause more motion-related side effects, but may still be the most effective medication for some patients. Second generation antipsychotics generally have less motion-related side effects, but still have side effects of their own. The medication that you or a loved one ends up taking will be largely decided through the symptoms that they experience and which side effects are least invasive in their life.

As for experiences with medication, I'm going to be honest. It can suck to take new meds, to wait them out to see how they affect you, and to possibly have to switch after all that effort. I have seen countless messages in psychosis-based communities of someone asking, "Does anyone have experiences with x medication?" And I understand. It can be scary to try new meds, and it is very easy to become discouraged. I will say, however, that trudging through these medications can be one of the most important sacrifices you ever make.

For many people, medications make the road to recovery easier. Easing symptoms sometimes allows for more clarity and can give some space to process what you're going through. It's crucial to let your doctor know exactly how your medications affect you. This can be really difficult! Personally, I talked to my family, friends, and partner about how they perceive my symptoms and disposition while I was on certain meds. I occasionally let them talk to my providers directly. This can help a lot, but it's also important to have your own insight into your experiences. Journaling can give you an account to look back on if you have trouble remembering your symptoms.

Antipsychotics can be tricky. The side effects vary from patient to patient, and medication to medication. They can take much longer than other medications to take effect, and it's important that they're taken consistently. Patience and honesty are vital to finding medications that work for you.

I'm hesitant to include this portion of the article, because most people have a different experience. For some patients, antipsychotics do not work as they should. Sometimes they don't ease symptoms in a patient, sometimes the side effects are too much for the patient. I strongly encourage you to talk with your doctor(s) and follow their recommendation. Medication is not a magic wand to heal you, but it can be a very effective tool. It is almost always used alongside therapy. It's not uncommon for antipsychotics to be used alongside other medications.

I'm writing this article because I think it's very important to be well-informed and to have a healthy view on medication. Psychiatric medication is constantly being developed. The medications on the market have helped countless people to have a life that they are happier with. However, happiness is a varied thing and if medications don't work for you, there are other options to help you recover. A good doctor (and/or therapist) will work with you based on your individual needs, goals, and experiences.

Too Long; Didn't Read: Antipsychotics have potential to help ease symptoms in psychotic patients. The medications that you take are specific to your needs, goals, and experiences. There is nothing wrong with taking medication that works for you. I wish you luck and hope this article helped you.
